### eval_001 — Require every new user to add at least one friend before they can reach session 2, so we seed the social graph early and lift long-term retention.

Verdict: **high** · pattern: forced action in onboarding

Cited: exp_010, exp_027, exp_038, exp_014

Summary: Mandatory friend-add before session 2 is a forced-action wall; near-identical prior attempts cratered retention.

Analysis:

> This is almost a carbon copy of [exp_010], the mandatory referral wall before session 2, which was killed on day 6 after Install-to-D7 fell 36.9% relative (p=0.0001), uninstall rate doubled, and the app-store rating dropped 0.7 points that took three months to recover. Your friend-add gate is structurally the same forced-action wall — friction dominated any "social anchoring" benefit by an order of magnitude there, and there is no reason to expect friend-add friction to behave differently. [exp_027] reinforces this: a far milder mandatory step (profile photo before first edit) still dropped D7 10.3% relative with 22.4% of users abandoning at the gate itself, and it explicitly flagged the causation-direction trap — engaged users add friends, not the reverse, so seeding the graph by force won't manufacture the retention you're attributing to it. [exp_038] adds the mechanism: blocking surfaces in onboarding are pure friction (the welcome modal's 64% dismissal rate lost head-to-head to a non-blocking email that lifted D7 6.4%). The genuine social-graph win, [exp_014], came from a non-mandatory following feed and scaled with graph depth (+6.1% D7, strongest in the 10+ followed cohort) — meaning the value is real but must be earned voluntarily, not gated. Kill the mandatory framing entirely; if you want to seed the graph, test an optional, dismissible friend-suggestion surface and measure whether depth of adoption predicts retention before assuming causation.

### eval_002 — Force new users to verify their phone number before their first edit — cuts spam accounts and improves trust in the following feed.

Verdict: **high** · pattern: forced action in onboarding

Cited: exp_027, exp_010, exp_001

Summary: Mandatory phone verification before first edit is a forced blocking action in onboarding, which the corpus has killed repeatedly.

Analysis:

> This proposal is a textbook forced-action-in-onboarding anti-pattern, and the corpus has punished it hard every time. [exp_027] made a profile photo mandatory before first edit and D7 dropped 10.3% relative (17.4% → 15.6%, p=0.0008), with 22.4% of users abandoning at the blocking step itself — phone verification is a heavier ask than a photo, so expect worse. [exp_010] is even more alarming: a mandatory referral wall cratered install-to-D7 by 36.9% (p=0.0001) and was killed on day 6, with app-store rating falling 0.7 points that took three months to recover — phone-number gating carries the same friction-dominates dynamic plus added regulatory and privacy exposure. Note also the causation trap from [exp_027]: the "trust in the following feed" motivation likely runs backwards, since engaged users complete verification, not the reverse, so a correlation between verified accounts and trust doesn't imply forcing verification creates trust. The one onboarding win in this set, [exp_001], came from removing friction with a 60-second fast-path (+7.9% D7), not adding it — that is the direction the evidence supports. If spam is the real problem, pursue it with a non-blocking or post-first-edit signal rather than a hard gate in the day-0 flow, and if you insist on testing this, ship it with the 30-day-post-D7 churn guardrail the post-exp_005 playbook now requires.
