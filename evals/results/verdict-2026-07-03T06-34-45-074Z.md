### eval_001 — Require every new user to add at least one friend before they can reach session 2, so we seed the social graph early and lift long-term retention.

Verdict: **high** · pattern: forced action in onboarding

Cited: exp_010, exp_027, exp_038, exp_014, exp_013

Summary: Near-exact repeat of exp_010's killed referral wall plus exp_027's mandatory-action pattern; expect double-digit D7 retention loss.

Analysis:

> This proposal is a near-carbon-copy of [exp_010], the 2024 mandatory referral wall that was killed on day 6 after Install-to-D7 fell 36.9% (p=0.0001), uninstalls doubled, and store rating dropped 0.7 points taking three months to recover — the exact same "social anchoring will offset forced-action friction" assumption that failed by an order of magnitude. [exp_027] then re-confirmed the pattern in a milder form: a mandatory profile photo (much lower friction than adding a friend) still dropped D7 by 10.3% with 22.4% abandoning at the forced step, and explicitly warned that the retention correlation motivating these ideas is reverse causation — engaged users add friends, forcing friend-adds does not create engaged users. [exp_038] adds a third confirming data point: blocking surfaces in onboarding underperform (modal was noise at +1.2%, p=0.41, with 64% dismissal), while the non-blocking email won at +6.4%. If the underlying goal is seeding the social graph, the corpus points to non-blocking alternatives: [exp_014]'s Following feed is already delivering +6.1% D7 for users with 3+ follows and lifts hardest at 10+ follows, suggesting social graph depth pays off when it's earned rather than forced. [exp_013]'s watermarked share (+42.9% K-factor) is another non-coercive graph-seeding lever with attested wins. My strong recommendation is to kill this framing before it runs and reformulate as an opt-in friend-suggestion surface post-first-edit, with the exp_005 30-day churn guardrail attached.

### eval_002 — Force new users to verify their phone number before their first edit — cuts spam accounts and improves trust in the following feed.

Verdict: **high** · pattern: forced action in onboarding

Cited: exp_010, exp_027, exp_001

Summary: Mandatory phone verification before first edit is a textbook forced-action-in-onboarding anti-pattern with two prior disasters.

Analysis:

> This proposal is a near-perfect structural match to the two worst outcomes in the retrieved set. [exp_010]'s mandatory referral wall cratered install-to-D7 by 36.9% (p=0.0001) and was killed on day 6 with a 0.7-point app-store rating hit that took three months to recover — phone verification is arguably a heavier ask than a referral tap, since it involves PII and SMS friction. [exp_027]'s mandatory profile photo dropped D7 by 10.3% (p=0.0008) with 22.4% abandoning at the gate itself; expect phone verification abandonment to be worse, since users can fake a profile photo in seconds but cannot fake an SMS. The spam/trust motivation here is also suspiciously similar to [exp_027]'s reasoning error — the correlation "verified users are more trustworthy" is almost certainly reverse causation, where already-engaged users tolerate verification, not the other way around. [exp_001] is the counter-example worth studying: it lifted D7 +7.9% by *removing* friction in onboarding, not adding it. If the real problem is feed spam, address it downstream (verification gated on posting/upload, or trust-score ranking in the feed) rather than blocking the first edit — the first-edit moment is the single most protected surface at Pixmate per the post-exp_010 and post-exp_027 playbooks. Recommend killing this framing pre-flight and reformulating with verification triggered at a later, higher-intent action.

### eval_003 — Ship a new push notification at hour 6 for users who installed but haven't opened the app, nudging them back in.

Verdict: **high** · pattern: push to silent new users burns permission budget

Cited: exp_003, exp_041, exp_020, exp_037, exp_017

Summary: Near-identical to killed exp_003; pushing silent installers historically burns permission budget without significant D7 lift.

Analysis:

> This proposal is structurally almost identical to [exp_003], which sent an 18-hour re-engagement push to silent new users and was killed: the +5.1% D7 lift didn't reach significance (p=0.21) and opt-out rate spiked 4.5x, with modeled long-term permission loss exceeding the retention gain within 60 days. A 6-hour push is likely worse, not better — you're pinging users who haven't even opened the app once, so they have no context for why Pixmate is notifying them, which is exactly what drove [exp_003]'s opt-out spike. [exp_041] reinforces this: it's currently cutting opt-out by 22.5% (34.2% for international users) specifically because the old scheduler was sending timezone-inappropriate pushes — a 6-hour trigger will fire at 3am for a meaningful slice of installs unless you gate it on quiet hours. The Lifecycle team's own pivot away from push toward in-app nudges paid off in [exp_020] (+4.9% D7, p=0.04) and [exp_037] (+5.6% D7, p=0.03), suggesting the productive lever for silent/low-engagement new users is contextual in-app surfaces on return, not more push. Also worth noting [exp_017]'s slot-displacement finding — even if this push "works," it competes with day-30+ winbacks and milestone sends in the priority queue. Recommend killing the push framing and instead testing a first-return in-app nudge for users whose first session was <30 seconds, or at minimum gating any push experiment behind [exp_041]'s quiet-hours logic and pre-committing to opt-out as a primary metric, not a guardrail.
