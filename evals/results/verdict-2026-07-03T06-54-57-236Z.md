### eval_001 — Require every new user to add at least one friend before they can reach session 2, so we seed the social graph early and lift long-term retention.

Verdict: **high** · pattern: forced action in onboarding

Cited: exp_010, exp_027, exp_038, exp_014, exp_018

Summary: Near-exact replay of a killed 2024 experiment; mandatory social action in onboarding is a well-documented anti-pattern here.

Analysis:

> This proposal is a near-literal replay of [exp_010], which forced a referral wall before session 2 and cratered Install-to-D7 by 36.9% (p=0.0001), doubled uninstalls, and cost 0.7 stars in app-store rating that took three months to recover — it was killed on day 6 of a 21-day run. The mechanism you're proposing (mandatory friend-add) is structurally identical: a blocking social action gating progression, betting that "social anchoring" will beat forced-action friction. [exp_027] then re-confirmed the pattern in a lower-stakes form: mandatory profile photo dropped D7 by 10.3% with 22.4% of users abandoning at the forced step itself, and importantly noted the causation likely runs the other way — engaged users add friends, not the reverse, which almost certainly applies to your hypothesis too. [exp_038] provides the constructive alternative: a non-blocking welcome email lifted D7 by 6.4% while the blocking modal was noise with a 64% dismissal rate, establishing that blocking surfaces in onboarding are friction, full stop. If seeding the social graph is the real goal, the supporting precedent is [exp_014], where the following feed lifted D7 by 6.1% for users with 3+ followed creators — meaning the lift comes from graph depth among willing users, not from coerced graph creation. Recommend killing this framing and reformulating as an opt-in friend-suggestion surface (email or non-blocking nudge) with the 30-day-post-D7 churn guardrail from the [exp_018] playbook attached.

### eval_002 — Force new users to verify their phone number before their first edit — cuts spam accounts and improves trust in the following feed.

Verdict: **high** · pattern: forced action in onboarding

Cited: exp_027, exp_010, exp_001

Summary: Mandatory phone verification before first edit is a textbook forced-action-in-onboarding pattern; two prior attempts cratered retention.

Analysis:

> Forcing phone verification before the first edit lands squarely on the forced-action-in-onboarding anti-pattern that [exp_027] and [exp_010] both established at high confidence — [exp_027]'s mandatory profile photo dropped D7 by 10.3% relative (p=0.0008) with 22.4% abandoning at the gate itself, and [exp_010]'s referral wall was killed on day 6 after install-to-D7 fell 37% (p=0.0001) and the app-store rating took three months to recover. Phone verification is heavier friction than either of those — it requires leaving the app for an SMS, exposes carrier/international failure modes, and the ask "give us your number to edit a photo" reads as a trust violation, not a trust signal. The framing that this "improves trust in the following feed" is the same causation-reversal trap [exp_027] fell into: verified users may correlate with better feed behavior because engaged users tolerate verification, not because verification produces engagement. The winning pattern from [exp_001] (+7.9% D7, p=0.03) is the opposite direction — get users to value before asking anything of them. If spam in the feed is the actual problem, solve it at the feed layer or gate verification at the point of first public post, not first edit; a first-edit block will almost certainly show a double-digit negative D7 lift and I would not approve this as written.

### eval_004 — Launch on-device AI portrait generation on all Android devices, including sub-4GB RAM tiers, to keep the feature free of server cost.

Verdict: **high** · pattern: on-device vision on low-RAM Android

Cited: exp_001, exp_022, exp_036

Summary: Corpus has twice hit crashes shipping on-device AI to sub-4GB Android; this proposal repeats that exact anti-pattern.

Analysis:

> This proposal walks directly into a named anti-pattern the corpus has hit twice. [exp_001] shipped an on-device AI portrait model in onboarding and tripped a hard crash-rate guardrail with a 62% crash spike on Android devices under 4GB RAM (roughly 32% of the Android base), forcing an iterated rollout. [exp_022] then repeated the pattern with a different on-device model — aggregate lift of +4.8% held up, but the sub-4GB Android cell showed minimal lift and a +8.8% crash-rate uptick, which the [exp_022] writeup explicitly flags as "the second time in 18 months" we've under-served the bottom third of Android with on-device AI. Shipping AI portrait generation — a heavier vision model than either of those precedents — to all Android tiers including sub-4GB is a near-certain third instance, and portrait generation OOMs will be more severe than the classifier in [exp_022]. The cost-avoidance framing is the wrong tradeoff to optimize against here: crash-rate guardrail breaches historically cost more in ASO and retention than server compute would, as [exp_036]'s brand-cost post-mortem underscores. Recommend gating on a RAM threshold (≥4GB) with a server-side fallback or a graceful "not supported on this device" state for the bottom tier, and running the experiment only on the eligible cell — otherwise this is a predictable guardrail breach, not a real test.
